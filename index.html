About

I am a researcher primarily interested in seeking human health. For this reason, I have dedicated myself to generating basic knowledge from molecular medicine research and to transferring this knowledge to clinical application through the development of innovation in the treatment of patients with various diseases (prostate cancer, benign prostatic hyperplasia, hypomagnesemia, and diabetic retinopathy). Since 2015, I have refocused my interests on the early diagnosis of preventable diseases, notably with changes in lifestyle, but not only.

I joined the Institute of Neurobiology from the National Autonomous University of Mexico in 2007, and started the Translational Research Laboratory in Visual Health in 2021. Over time, thanks to the hard work of our entire team, we introduced a new modality for the non-invasive electroretinogram (ERG) technique to record the basal or resting electrical activity of the retina and use this signal for the timely diagnosis of sight-threatening retinopathies, like diabetic retinopathy and diabetic macular edema, and to test new treatments for retinitis pigmentosa.

Our experimental approach is interdisciplinary: we combine physiological, pharmacological, psychophysical, and bioinformatics approaches to understand the function of retinal circuits in resting conditions. We use molecular techniques to genetically target specific molecules in the retinal network. We juggle between experimental models and human studies, depending on the questions we have to solve. In particular, we collaborate with local clinicians at IMO, with optometrists in Mexico and Canada, vision scientists, and psychiatrists in France.

Currently, we are focusing on the use of basal ERG signals for renal and brain diseases, and the introduction of microscopic imaging to in vivo ERG measurements. The results of our research also led us to implement a prevention program called "No More Complications with Diabetes" in the Eye Health Optometry clinic of the ENES Le√≥n Unit of the UNAM, where, among others, the non-invasive basal ERG test is used to predict the risk of developing metabolic syndrome, which is the main risk factor for type 2 diabetes, in children and adults.
